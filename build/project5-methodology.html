Methodology1 · Aggregation & building blocks

1 The one rule, and the building blocks

Read this first — it governs every single number in every file.

The aggregation rule: value-weighted, never an average of ratios

For any industry ratio, Damodaran sums the numerator across all firms, sums the denominator, then divides. His FAQ repeats the phrase "aggregated … as a percent of aggregated …" for every metric.

Industry ratio = Σ(numerator, all firms) / Σ(denominator, all firms) e.g. Gross Margin = Σ(gross profit) / Σ(revenue)

Consequence: each figure is a value-weighted composite dominated by the largest firms — the "industry as one merged company," not the typical firm. (He even labels the count column's rationale "Law of large numbers?".)

The building blocks (defined once, reused everywhere)

TermDamodaran's definition

Why two of these get "adjusted"

Damodaran argues two things are mis-classified by accounting: R&D (really an investment, but expensed) and operating leases (really debt, but hidden). He re-computes operating income and invested capital to fix both — the next two sections. Every "adjusted" column in the margin file flows from these two corrections.

2 Capitalizing R&D — the centerpiece

This is the whole basis of the margin-distortion thesis. Here is exactly how he does it.

GAAP expenses R&D immediately. Damodaran instead treats it as a capital asset amortized over an industry-specific life (longer for pharma, shorter for software — matching how long the research stays commercially valuable). His FAQ states a 5-year straight-line default.

Step 1 — build the "Research Asset" (the unamortized R&D)

With a 5-year life, this year's R&D counts fully; last year's is 4/5 remaining; and so on:

Research Asset = R&D₀ + ⅝·R&D₋₁ + ⅜·R&D₋₂ + ⅙·R&D₋₃ + ⅛·R&D₋₄ (this is the "R&D Capitalized" column; "R&D 1–5 years ago" are the inputs)

Step 2 — re-state operating income (his exact FAQ formula)

R&D FAQ: "R&D adjustment to operating income = Operating Income + Current year's R&D exp − Amortization of R&D asset."
Adjusted EBIT = EBIT + R&D(current) − R&D amortization amortization ≈ ⅕ of each of the last five years' R&D (≈ the trailing average)
The mechanism that explains our result

The reported→adjusted gap = (current R&D − amortization) / revenue. If R&D is growing, current > amortization → the adjustment raises the margin (in our data, Software 0.330 → 0.344). If R&D is large but flat/mature, amortization of the big accumulated asset ≈ current → little or negative change (Pharma 0.295 → 0.272). That's precisely why the distortion concentrates in the high-R&D-growth tail.

Step 3 (used elsewhere): the Research Asset is also added to invested capital, which lowers measured Return on Capital — the Capitalized R&D as % of Invested Capital column.

3 Capitalizing leases, and every margin column

The second adjustment, then the full margin.xls dictionary with exact formulas.

The lease adjustment (his exact FAQ formula)

Margin FAQ: "Lease adjustment to operating income = Operating Income + Current year's lease exp − Lease Asset Depreciation. Lease expenses are financing, not operating expenses. Hence we add them back."

Operating leases are treated as debt (a lease liability = present value of future commitments), which creates a leased "asset" that is depreciated. Note: after ASC 842 / IFRS 16 (~2019) leases already sit on the balance sheet, shrinking this adjustment — which is why cross-year comparisons need care.

The thesis in one line of algebra

"Adjusted" − "reported" operating margin = (current R&D − R&D amortization + lease add-back) / revenue. Ranking industries by the reported line therefore understates exactly the R&D- and lease-heavy ones — the gap our project measures.

A verified contradiction — trust the data, not the note

The finflows FAQ says "Net Equity Change = Stock Issuances − Buybacks − Dividends." But the published numbers do not subtract dividends. We checked row-by-row: Advertising = 1996.0 − 2180.4 = −184.3 (the value in the file); subtracting dividends would give −755.4. So the real formula is Issuance − Buybacks. We document the true one and compute "net payout including dividends" separately when needed.

"Where is the EPS calculation?" — three places, none of them a standalone column

1 · Inside PE. Damodaran doesn't publish an "industry EPS." He computes PE = price per share ÷ EPS for each firm, then averages across money-making firms — so EPS is the denominator of every PE column (EPS = net income ÷ shares).

2 · Money-losers are dropped. His FAQ warns simple-average PEs exclude negative-EPS firms ("that will create a bias"); the Aggregate Mkt Cap / Net Income columns are the value-weighted alternative that keeps them.

3 · EPS growth is a separate file. Expected EPS growth in pedata is analyst-based; the fundamental EPS growth formula lives in fundgr.xls — see §6. (We loaded its operating-income sibling, fundgrEB.xls.)

6 Payout, FCFE & the two growth formulas

divfcfe.xls and the fundamental-growth files — the second answers the EPS-growth question.

divfcfe.xls — cash returned vs. cash that could be returned

ColumnFormula (Damodaran's wording)
PayoutΣ dividends / Σ net income
Cash ReturnΣ (dividends + buybacks) / Σ net income
FCFE, before debt cash flowsΣ (net income + depreciation − capex incl. R&D & acquisitions)
FCFE, after debt cash flows…+ Σ (new debt raised − debt repaid)
Net Cash Returns as % of FCFE(div + buybacks − net issuance) / FCFE

FCFE is "potential dividends" — cash left after taxes, reinvestment and debt flows (Damodaran calls the before-debt version "Owner's earnings"). Net-cash-returned > 100% of FCFE flags an industry paying out more than it generates.

The two fundamental-growth formulas

Growth = how much you reinvest × how well it earns. There's an equity version (per-share earnings) and an operating version (EBIT):

EPS growth (fundgr.xls) = Retention Ratio × ROE Retention = 1 − payout ; ROE = net income / BV equity EBIT growth (fundgrEB.xls) = Reinvestment Rate × ROC ← the file we loaded Reinvestment = (net capex incl. R&D & acq. + Δ non-cash WC) / EBIT(1−t) ROC = EBIT(1−t) / Invested Capital
Direct answer to the EPS-growth question

The EPS growth calculation is Retention Ratio × ROE, published in fundgr.xls. Our project loaded the operating-income analogue, fundgrEB.xls (Reinvestment × ROC), because the thesis is about operating margins, not per-share earnings. Both are the same idea — reinvestment × return — one for equity, one for the whole firm.

Reading these numbers7 · Caveats

7 Caveats baked into the calculations

What to keep in mind when you use any of the formulas above.

  • They're estimates where it counts. The R&D amortizable life, the lease discount rate, and the effective-vs-marginal tax choice are Damodaran's assumptions — the capitalized-R&D and adjusted margins are model outputs, not GAAP figures.
  • Value-weighted ≠ typical firm. Every ratio is dominated by the industry's mega-caps. Nothing here supports a claim about an individual company.
  • Negative / near-zero denominators distort ratios. Book equity can go negative after years of buybacks; net income can be negative — so %-of-book and PE columns blow up. He publishes money-makers-only PE and "without financials" totals for this reason; we winsorise downstream.
  • Financials are special. Banks/insurers have no comparable "revenue" or "operating margin," so they're excluded from margin/ROC computations.
  • One snapshot. All trailing-12-months through ~Q3 of the prior year — a single point in the cycle, not a trend. The "N years ago" columns are computed on today's survivors (survivorship bias).
  • Definitions can lag the data — the Net Equity Change contradiction (§4) is the proof; always sanity-check a formula against a couple of rows.
How this feeds the project

Our pipeline reads these columns as-is into a database and computes two things on top: the reported-vs-adjusted margin gap (from the two adjustments in §2–3) and whether the market's EV/Sales (§5) tracks the reported or adjusted margin. Everything upstream is Damodaran's arithmetic; we don't re-derive it — we compare its outputs.
